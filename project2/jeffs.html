Initial Prototype of Your Web Interface

In this assignment, you’ll take on the role of a requester by creating tasks on Amazon Mechanical Turk, and submitting your first HIT “programmatically” -- that is, without using the GUI provided on the Amazon Mechanical Turk site. To me, this is one of the first steps of really “programming the crowd” because it demonstrates the start of being able to write programs that automatically draw in human intelligence when it’s needed.

A HIT (or task page) is just a web page, so we’ll start by creating a web page for your task. A web page with that contains a form that will be submitted to Mechanical Turk with a specific format. You might find it useful to include the script “mturk.js” that is also included on that site. Similar to the Javascript that we developed in class, that script will help to set your page up for Amazon Mechanical Turk.

Creating the Web Page for your HIT

At minimum your web page needs to show the image and provide a text box so that workers can enter how many people they see.

The challenge of the assignment is to design an interface that collects high-quality data from workers. You’ll note that while some of the photos have a fairly obvious (and quick) answer, others will be more difficult. (We’ll decide as a class how many people are in each photo).

There is no set guidelines on what you need to include in your web page for collecting HITs, but you might consider some of the lessons we’ve learned in lecture and from the readings. For instance, how can the interface encourage high-quality responses? How can you make it as difficult to do this job poorly as to do it well? Could you use a gold standard question? How can you write your instructions clearly? Can you build in some other quality control mechanism? Maybe you can pay them a high amount, or maybe you will bonus them if they agree with others?

The only rule is that you have to use the same interface for each of the images (in order to best reflect how an interface like this would be used in a real setting in which you can’t know in advance how many people are in each image or what kinds of images they are).

You will be graded in part on the accuracy of your workers, so put time and thought into how to encourage them to give you high-quality input (20% of your final grade). This will be calculated based on the input they provide.

You will get two chances for feedback on your interface from your classmates -- first on 9/25, and then again on 10/2. You’ll need to have a prototype ready on each of these class days in order to get feedback.

Preparing Your Web Page for Mechanical Turk

As a real test of the quality of your interface, you will post your HITs to Mechanical Turk and collect responses. Be sure to sign up as a requester early!

We suggest hosting your HITs on Github Pages. The reason is that it’s free, relatively easy, and supports https, which Mechanical Turk requires. It’s not fancy web hosting. You’ll have ~zero control over the backend. But, as we’ll see, almost everything we’ll be doing is on the front-end anyway.

Testing Out Your HIT

The first step to getting your web-based task up on Mechanical Turk is to become a requester. Visit the following page to register as a requester:  requester.mturk.com You may also have to register on the requester sandbox, at requestersandbox.mturk.com.

Next, you’ll need to prepare your environment to programmatically post your job. We’ve prepared some easy tools to for you to use to post your jobs on Mechanical Turk programatically and made them available at http://programthecrowd.com/tools/. These are based on the popular Boto framework. Please download the zip file at that location and follow the instruction in the README file to setup boto and create a HIT.

After downloading the tools, you’ll want to modify the files to use your Mechanical Turk Requester credentials.  There are some great instructions on getting started with Mechanical Turk that overviews this process. One step that you’ll need to do is to get your security credentials and put them into the Python scripts we’ve provided. There’s an excerpt from the AWS guide below that shows this:

You should be able to modify the example that you downloaded in order to post your own HITs to Mechanical Turk. We strongly recommend posting and debugging your HITs on the Mechanical Turk sandbox as described in class before posting on the real site and potentially using real money.

When setting the wage for your work, we strongly encourage you to do your best to make it a living wage. That is a minimum of $6/hour. Many workers will not work if the pay works out to less than $10/hour. Since you built your interface, it’s probably really easy for you to use, but it may take someone new to it longer to figure out. Please try your interface with someone else (and err on the high side) when setting wages.

We expect that you’ll spend between $5 and $10. There’s not an easy way for us to reimburse you for this, but Jeff will buy the class lunch one day…and, after assignment 1 you now have a (somewhat) easy way to make money!

What to submit

You’ll submit four things:

1.  a Google Document answer the questions below

2.  the URL of your HIT page that you posted to Mechanical Turk

3.  a Google Spreadsheet of your data in the format described below

1.  Google Document Questions

Questions to provide short responses to:

1.  Briefly describe your design. What did you do to encourage high-quality inputs? What did you change from your initial prototype to your final one?

2.  Do you think you got good data back from workers?

3.  How much did it cost you to complete your experiments?

4.  Include a screenshot listing your completed HITs

2.  URL of the page you posted to Mechanical Turk

The URL of the page you posted to Mechanical Turk. The web page should be viewable / usable even if not viewed through the Mechanical Turk interface. One way to do this is to only disable page elements if assignmentID==”ASSIGNMENT_ID_NOT_AVAILABLE”

3.  Data

Submit the URL to a Google Spreadsheet containing your data in the following format:

Columns: workerid, image1, image2, image3, image4, image5, image6, image7

There should be 12 rows. 1 row at the top with the column headings, 10 rows containing the counts entered by each worker and their worker id, and 1 row at the end labeled “correct” with the correct answer provided.
